Most people think meditation is complicated. It isn't.

You don't need incense. You don't need a special cushion. You don't need to sit cross-legged or empty your mind. (That last one is actually impossible — and trying to do it is the number one reason people quit.)

Here's everything you need to start meditating today.

## What Meditation Actually Is

Meditation is the practice of paying attention on purpose.

That's it. You're training your attention — the same way you'd train a muscle — to stay focused on one thing rather than bouncing around between every passing thought, worry, or notification.

The "thing" you focus on is usually your breath. Not because your breath is magical, but because it's always with you, always in the present moment, and it gives your wandering mind something to come back to.

## Why Your Mind Wanders (And Why That's Fine)

Here's the part nobody tells beginners: your mind will wander. Every time you sit down to meditate.

After five seconds, you'll be thinking about your to-do list. Or something someone said two years ago. Or what you want for lunch.

This is not failure. This is the practice.

The moment you notice your mind has wandered — that moment of noticing is the entire point. You're building the muscle of awareness. Each time you notice and return to your breath, you've done one "rep."

A session where your mind wanders 47 times and you return 47 times is just as valuable as one where you feel completely calm.

## The 5-Step Beginner Method

You can do this right now. It takes 5 minutes.

**Step 1: Find a comfortable position**

Sit in a chair with your feet flat on the floor, or sit on the floor cross-legged — whatever is comfortable. You want your back relatively straight (it helps you stay alert) but you don't need to be rigid.

**Step 2: Set a timer for 5 minutes**

Use Still or any timer. Starting with 5 minutes removes the question "how much longer?" from your mind so you can actually focus.

**Step 3: Close your eyes and take three deep breaths**

Breathe in slowly through your nose, hold for a moment, exhale through your mouth. Do this three times. This signals to your nervous system that it's time to slow down.

**Step 4: Let your breath return to normal and focus on it**

Stop controlling your breath. Just notice it. The sensation of air entering your nostrils. Your chest or belly rising and falling. Pick one sensation and put your attention there.

**Step 5: When your mind wanders, return**

You'll notice a thought. That's fine. Don't judge it, don't follow it. Just notice it like a cloud passing, and gently return your attention to your breath.

Repeat until the timer goes off.

## How Long to Meditate

For beginners, 5 minutes is better than 20 minutes done inconsistently.

The research on meditation benefits shows that consistency matters more than duration. Five minutes every day for 30 days will do more than 30 minutes twice a week.

Once 5 minutes feels easy (usually around day 7–10), try 10 minutes. The Still app guides you through this progression automatically — sessions are designed to grow with you as your practice deepens.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

**"I'm not doing it right because my mind won't stop."**
Your mind never stops. That's not the goal. See step 5.

**"I fell asleep."**
This usually means you're tired. Try meditating at a different time of day, or sit up a little straighter.

**"I don't feel anything."**
Most sessions feel unremarkable. The benefits accumulate in the background. You'll notice them in everyday moments — a slightly longer pause before you react, a little more ease with discomfort.

**"I missed a day and gave up."**
Missing days is normal. The streak isn't the practice. Come back tomorrow.

## What Happens When You Keep Going

After about two weeks of daily practice, most people notice small changes:
- Slightly more space between a trigger and a reaction
- A bit more ease with boredom or waiting
- Marginally better sleep quality
- A new ability to notice when their mind is spinning

These aren't dramatic transformations. They're subtle shifts that compound over time.
